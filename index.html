Introduction

What is algorithmic bias
  • When AI systems produce outcomes that unfairly advantage or disadvantage groups.
  • Sources include biased training data, design choices that maximise engagement over fairness, and how problems or datasets are framed.
  • Example: A commercial image tool mislabelled Black people as gorillas, showing how data and design can reflect prejudice.

Why it matters in education

  • AI is embedded in assessment tools, learning analytics, and content recommendations.
  • Risks include mislabelling students as at risk, unfair grading, and skewed worldviews through feeds.

Curriculum signal

The Australian Curriculum highlights equity, unconscious bias, ethics and values when engaging with technology.

Key message: Understanding AI bias is core digital citizenship. Help students see AI as fallible, not neutral. Encourage fairness, transparency, and critical questioning.

Literature and best practice

Causes of algorithmic bias
  • Biased training data can embed historical inequalities and reproduce them in outputs.
  • Design and optimisation goals may amplify stereotypes when engagement or accuracy is prioritised without safeguards.
  • Types: allocative bias affects opportunities, representational bias distorts portrayals.
Impacts on education
  • Learners: misidentification of ability and disproportionate risk flags undermine trust.
  • System level: when tools work better for majority groups, gaps can widen.
  • Youth lenses: filter bubbles shape worldviews and reduce exposure to diversity.
Best practice for teaching
  • Algorithm auditing as student inquiry to demystify AI and build data literacy.
  • Case studies like the UK 2020 grading algorithm or Amazon hiring AI prompt fairness and accountability analysis.
  • Critical media analysis tasks such as comparing search terms that expose stereotypes.
Key insight

Bias is not only technical but social. Make it visible through concrete, hands-on classroom inquiry.

Ethical and eSafety

Data ethics and privacy

  • Interrogate data collection. Is it necessary. Is it fair.
  • Class activity: review a learning app privacy policy or audit device data collection.

Fairness and accountability

  • Ask who benefits and who is disadvantaged.
  • Introduce simple fairness checks with diverse groups.

Student welfare and eSafety

  • Discuss recommender risks and how to challenge harmful feeds.
  • Strategy: audit your own feed and reflect on what is hidden.
Safety by Design

Promote early bias reduction and risk mitigation in tools you adopt for class.

Implementation challenges

Teacher knowledge and confidence

Use short PL, peer collaboration, and scaffolded language to avoid oversimplification.

Student misconceptions

Tackle automation bias with relatable examples and a culture of questioning.

Resources and time

Integrate with existing units. Use unplugged activities and free demos. Start small.

Inclusive dialogue

Set norms. Handle sensitive topics with care. Partner with wellbeing staff where needed.

Teacher recommendations

Activity idea 1: Search bias mini-lab

  1. In pairs, compare results for two terms like professional hairstyle vs unprofessional hairstyle.
  2. Annotate patterns. Who is represented. Who is missing.
  3. Discuss social assumptions and how AI learns them.

Activity idea 2: Recommender audit

  1. Use two clean profiles or two devices.
  2. Interact differently for 10 minutes and log recommended content.
  3. Reflect on pathways and risks. Plan mitigation strategies.
Assessment tip

Use curriculum language in task sheets. Example: evaluate reliability and bias in an AI generated source. Apply privacy principles to recommend improvements.
